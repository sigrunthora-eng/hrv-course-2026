ACTIVITY 1 REAL INTRODUCTIONS
⏱ 12 min

Why real introductions matter: Teams with higher psychological safety — where members know each other as people — show greater creativity and fewer blind spots (Edmondson, 1999). Question 3 models that limitations are safe to name.

Edmondson, A. (1999). Psychological safety and learning behavior in work teams. Administrative Science Quarterly, 44(2), 350–383.

ACTIVITY 3 TEAM CHARTER
⏱ 12 min

Team charters reduce conflict: Agreeing on norms early significantly reduces misunderstandings later. Teams that set explicit expectations at the start report higher cohesion and lower dropout (Hackman, 2002).

Hackman, J.R. (2002). Leading teams. Harvard Business School Press.

ACTIVITY 4 CHECK-IN PAIRS
⏱ 10 min

Check-in partners increase follow-through: Having a named partner who will ask "Did you do your practice?" activates social accountability. Regular brief check-ins are more effective than accountability to a group or instructor (Prestwich et al., 2012).

Prestwich, A., et al. (2012). Randomized controlled trial of collaborative implementation intentions. Health Psychology, 31(1), 67–75.

Setting up check-in pairs
  • Two pairs of 2 people each.
  • Between meetings: one brief message, one question — 'Did you do your HRV practice?'
  • Agree now on WHEN and HOW (WhatsApp / Teams / text).
  • Facilitator: ask each pair directly in the main call — takes 2 minutes.

ACTIVITY B HRV PRACTICE: FIRST REFLECTIONS
⏱ 10 min

Resonance frequency breathing: Breathing at ~6 breaths/min (4s in, 6s out) maximises heart rate variability by synchronising respiratory and cardiac rhythms. Regular practice (20 min/day) is associated with reduced anxiety, better stress regulation, and improved autonomic flexibility.

Lehrer, P., & Gevirtz, R. (2014). Heart rate variability biofeedback. Frontiers in Psychology, 5, 756.

Go-around
  • Each person: How often did you practice this week? (0–7 days)
  • What did you notice? One sentence.
  • No evaluation — just observation.
ACTIVITY C STATUS UPDATE FROM CHECK-IN PAIRS
⏱ 12 min

Vicarious mastery (Bandura, 1997): Observing someone similar to you succeed increases your own self-efficacy. When peers share specific moments where the practice worked, it raises the group's collective belief that it is achievable — more powerfully than instructor demonstrations.

Bandura, A. (1997). Self-efficacy: The exercise of control. Freeman.

Status update from each check-in pair — what came up between meetings?

ACTIVITY D BARRIERS & SUPPORT — MI PEER APPROACH
⏱ 10 min

Motivational Interviewing principles: MI uses open questions, reflective listening, and affirming change talk. When peers (not just instructors) ask "What would make this easier?" the effect on motivation is amplified — people feel heard, not lectured.

Miller, W.R., & Rollnick, S. (2013). Motivational interviewing (3rd ed.). Guilford Press.

Open questions (pick 1–2)
  • What makes it harder than you expected?
  • What would make it easier?
  • On a scale 0–10, how important is this to you right now?

APP C
EVIDENCE BASE
Psychological frameworks underpinning this workbook

HRV Biofeedback

Breathing at resonance frequency (~6 breaths/min) maximises heart rate variability. Regular practice (≥20 min/day) reduces anxiety, improves stress regulation, and increases autonomic flexibility.

Lehrer, P., & Gevirtz, R. (2014). Heart rate variability biofeedback: How and why does it work? Frontiers in Psychology, 5, 756.

Lehrer, P., et al. (2020). Heart rate variability biofeedback improves emotional and physical health. Applied Psychophysiology and Biofeedback, 45, 109–129.

Motivational Interviewing (MI)

Open-ended questions, reflective listening, and affirming change talk — when applied by peers (not just clinicians) — significantly increase intrinsic motivation and behaviour change.

Miller, W.R., & Rollnick, S. (2013). Motivational interviewing: Helping people change (3rd ed.). Guilford Press.

Vicarious Mastery (Bandura)

Observing a similar person succeed raises your own self-efficacy more than instruction alone. Peer sharing of specific mastery moments is more persuasive than statistics.

Bandura, A. (1997). Self-efficacy: The exercise of control. Freeman.

Social Accountability & Check-in Partners

Named partners who ask "Did you practice?" activate social accountability. Brief, regular check-ins are more effective than periodic reviews or group accountability.

Prestwich, A., et al. (2012). Randomized controlled trial of collaborative implementation intentions. Health Psychology, 31(1), 67–75.

Co-Regulation & Shared Breathing

Breathing in synchrony activates shared physiological rhythms that reduce interpersonal stress and build social cohesion — the neuroscience of why the opening ritual matters.

Feldman Barrett, L. (2017). How emotions are made. Houghton Mifflin Harcourt.

Public Commitment & Follow-Through

Stating a specific commitment out loud in front of peers increases follow-through significantly compared to private intention. Specificity (when, where, how) is key.

Gollwitzer, P.M. (1999). Implementation intentions. American Psychologist, 54(7), 493–503.
